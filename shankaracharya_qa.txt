Q: Who was Adi Shankaracharya?
A: Adi Shankaracharya was a great Indian philosopher and theologian who consolidated the doctrine of Advaita Vedanta. He lived in the 8th century and is credited with unifying and establishing the main currents of thought in Hinduism.

Q: When and where was Adi Shankaracharya born?
A: Adi Shankaracharya was born in Kalady, a village in present-day Kerala, India, around 788 CE.

Q: What does Advaita Vedanta mean?
A: Advaita Vedanta is a non-dualistic school of Hindu philosophy which teaches that the individual soul (Atman) and the ultimate reality (Brahman) are one and the same.

Q: What are the main works of Adi Shankaracharya?
A: Some of his most important works include commentaries on the Brahma Sutras, the Bhagavad Gita, and the principal Upanishads. He also wrote original texts like Vivekachudamani, Atma Bodha, and Bhaja Govindam.

Q: What is the significance of Vivekachudamani?
A: Vivekachudamani, meaning "The Crest Jewel of Discrimination," is a philosophical treatise that elaborates on the nature of reality, the self, and the path to liberation.

Q: How did Shankaracharya influence Hinduism?
A: He played a key role in reviving Hinduism at a time when it was in decline and facing threats from other ideologies. He traveled across India, debating scholars and promoting the teachings of Advaita Vedanta.

Q: How many mathas did Shankaracharya establish?
A: Adi Shankaracharya established four mathas (monastic centers) in the four cardinal directions of India: Sringeri (South), Dwaraka (West), Puri (East), and Jyotirmath (North).

Q: What is the purpose of the four mathas?
A: The four mathas were established to preserve and spread the teachings of Advaita Vedanta and to promote spiritual education and Vedic scholarship.

Q: What is Shankaracharya’s philosophy of Maya?
A: According to Shankaracharya, Maya is the illusion that causes individuals to perceive the world as separate from Brahman. Realizing the truth of non-duality dispels this illusion and leads to liberation.

Q: What was his view on Moksha?
A: Shankaracharya taught that Moksha, or liberation, is the realization of one’s true identity with Brahman, which transcends all duality and worldly bondage.

Q: Did Adi Shankaracharya compose hymns?
A: Yes, he composed many devotional hymns like Bhaja Govindam, Soundarya Lahari, and Nirvana Shatakam that are still widely recited and revered.

Q: What is Nirvana Shatakam?
A: Nirvana Shatakam is a six-verse Sanskrit poem summarizing the essence of Advaita Vedanta and the realization of the self as Brahman.

Q: Who were his disciples?
A: His principal disciples were Padmapada, Totakacharya, Hastamalaka, and Sureshwaracharya. They helped spread his teachings and led the mathas.

Q: What happened during Shankaracharya’s debate with Mandana Mishra?
A: In a legendary debate, Adi Shankaracharya debated the scholar Mandana Mishra. After several days of intense discussion, Mandana accepted defeat and became one of Shankaracharya’s foremost disciples.

Q: At what age did Shankaracharya attain Samadhi?
A: He is believed to have attained Mahasamadhi at the age of 32 in Kedarnath, located in the Himalayas.

Q: What is Shankaracharya's legacy today?
A: His teachings continue to shape modern Hindu thought. The mathas he founded remain influential, and his works are studied by philosophers and seekers across the world.

Q: Can you quote a famous teaching of Shankaracharya?
A: One of his famous quotes is: "Brahma Satyam Jagan Mithya, Jivo Brahmaiva Na Parah" — meaning "Brahman is the only truth, the world is illusory, and the individual self is none other than Brahman."

Q: What is Bhaja Govindam about?
A: Bhaja Govindam is a devotional hymn urging people to seek God and realize the impermanence of worldly pleasures.

Q: How did Shankaracharya travel across India?
A: He is said to have traveled extensively on foot, engaging in debates, teaching, and establishing institutions to revive Vedic traditions.

Q: What is the relevance of Adi Shankaracharya in modern times?
A: His emphasis on unity, rational philosophy, and spiritual awakening continues to inspire scholars, spiritual leaders, and practitioners of Vedanta around the world.
